Short description

Zhmurki is a darkly comic, violent portrait of the post-Soviet 1990s. The story follows two small-time criminals who, after a bungled job, are sent on a string of absurd and brutal errands. The film blends slapstick-like sequences with sudden bursts of cruelty, turning the chaos of the era into a grotesque, satirical "game."

The title refers to a children's game similar to "blind man's buff" — an ironic lens through which the film examines blind greed, moral blindness, and the unpredictability of life during the "wild 90s."

Director

Aleksei Balabanov — a filmmaker known for mixing bleak humor with raw depictions of Russian life. In Zhmurki Balabanov uses exaggerated characters and violent set pieces to critique social collapse and moral confusion.

Notable traits
satirical tone, abrupt violence, morally ambiguous characters

Why this film matters (brief)

As both a period piece and a genre parody, the film captures the absurdity and lawlessness of a transitional moment in Russian history. Its mix of humor and horror leaves a lingering impression — the audience is invited to laugh, then immediately confront the consequences.
