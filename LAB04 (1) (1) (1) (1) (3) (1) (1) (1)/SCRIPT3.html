**Mikhail Bulgakov**

**Morphine**

**I**

Wise men have long observed that happiness is like health: when you have it, you don't notice it. But when the years have passed—oh, how you remember happiness, oh, how you remember!

As for me, it turns out now that I was happy in the winter of 1917. That unforgettable, blizzard-ridden, headlong year!

The blizzard that had begun caught me up like a scrap of torn newspaper and carried me from a remote district to a county town. A county town—a great thing, you might think? But if anyone, like me, has spent a winter sitting in the snow, summers in the harsh and impoverished forests, for a year and a half, without leaving for a single day; if anyone has torn open the wrapper of a week-old newspaper with as much palpitation of the heart as a happy lover tears open a blue envelope; if anyone has travelled eighteen versts by sleigh, in single-harness, to attend a birth—that person, I suppose, will understand me.

A kerosene lamp is a most cozy thing, but I am for electricity!

And so I saw them again at last, those seductive electric light bulbs! The main street of the little town, well-packed by peasants' sleighs; a street on which, charming the eye, hung a sign with boots, a golden pretzel, red flags, and the image of a young man with piggy, insolent little eyes and an absolutely unnatural hairstyle, signifying that behind the glass doors was housed the local Basile, who for thirty kopeks would undertake to shave you at any time, except on holidays, of which my fatherland abounds.

To this day I remember with a shudder Basile's napkins—napkins that incessantly conjured up that page in the German textbook of skin diseases where, with convincing clarity, a hard chancre is depicted on the chin of some citizen.

But even those napkins will not darken my memories!

At the crossroads stood a live militiaman; in a dusty shop window, sheets of tin with tight rows of pastries sporting ginger cream were dimly visible; hay covered the square; and people walked, and drove, and talked; in a kiosk they sold yesterday's Moscow newspapers, containing astounding news; not far off, Moscow trains whistled invitingly back and forth. In a word, this was civilization, Babylon, Nevsky Prospekt.

And the hospital—there was no comparison. It had a surgical department, a therapeutic department, an isolation ward, and a maternity ward. The hospital had an operating room, in which shone an autoclave, taps gleamed with silver, and tables revealed their cunning paws, cogs, and screws. The hospital had a head physician, three house surgeons (apart from me), feldshers, midwives, nurses, a pharmacy, and a laboratory. A laboratory, just think of it! With a Zeiss microscope, a splendid supply of stains.

I shuddered and grew cold, crushed by the impressions. Many days passed before I grew accustomed to the fact that in the December twilight the one-story hospital buildings, as if on command, lit up with electric light.

It dazzled me. In the baths, water raged and thundered, and battered wooden thermometers dove and floated in them. In the children's isolation ward, groans flared up all day, a thin, pitiful crying could be heard, a hoarse gurgling...

Nurses ran, rushed about...

A heavy burden slipped from my soul. I no longer bore the fateful responsibility for everything that might happen in the world. I was not to blame for a strangulated hernia, and I didn't flinch when a sleigh arrived bringing a woman with a transverse presentation; purulent pleurisies requiring surgery were not my concern... For the first time I felt like a man whose sphere of responsibility was limited by some bounds. A birth?—Please, over there—the low building, over there—the far window draped with white gauze. That's where the obstetrician is, a pleasant, stout man with a little reddish mustache and balding. That's his business. Sleigh-driver, turn towards the window with the gauze! A complicated fracture?—The head surgeon. Pneumonia?—To the therapeutic department, to Pavel Vladimirovich.

Oh, majestic machine of a large hospital running on well-oiled, precisely lubricated wheels! Like a new screw made to a pre-taken measure, I entered the apparatus and took charge of the children's department. And diphtheria and scarletina consumed me, occupied my days. But only the days. I began to sleep at night, because I no longer heard beneath my window the ominous nocturnal knocking that could rouse me and drag me off into the darkness towards danger and inevitability. In the evenings I began to read (about diphtheria and scarletina, of course, first and foremost, and then, for some reason, Fenimore Cooper with a strange interest) and fully appreciated the lamp on the table, the grey coals on the samovar's tray, the cooling tea, and sleep after a year and a half of sleeplessness...

That is how happy I was in the winter of '17, having been transferred from a remote, blizzard-bound district to a county town.

**II**

A month flew by, then a second and a third; the year 1917 passed, and February of 1918 flew in. I grew accustomed to my new position and little by little began to forget my distant district. The green lamp with its hissing kerosene, the loneliness, the snowdrifts were erased from memory... Ungrateful that I am! I had forgotten my fighting post, where I alone, without any support, struggled against diseases, with my own strength, like a hero of Fenimore Cooper, extricating myself from the most outlandish situations.

Occasionally, it is true, as I lay in bed with the pleasant thought of how I would soon fall asleep, some fragments would flash through my already darkening consciousness. A green light, a flickering lantern... the creak of a sleigh... a short groan, then darkness, the muffled howl of a blizzard in the fields... Then all this would tumble sideways and disappear...

"I wonder who's sitting in my place now?... Someone must be... A young doctor like me... Well, I've served my time. February, March, April... well, and let's say, May—and the end of my practical service. So, at the end of May I'll part with my brilliant city and return to Moscow. And if the revolution catches me up on its wing—I might have to travel some more... but, in any case, I will never see my district again... Never... The capital... A clinic... Asphalt, lights...."

Thus I thought.

"...And still, it's good that I worked in the district... I've become a courageous man... I'm not afraid... What diseases haven't I treated?! Indeed? Huh?.. I haven't treated mental illnesses... Wait... no, let's see... The agronomist drank himself to the point of seeing devils... And I treated him, and rather unsuccessfully... Delirium tremens...

Isn't that a mental illness? I should read up on psychiatry... Oh, forget it... Somehow, later in Moscow... But now, first and foremost, children's diseases... and still children's diseases... and especially this damned children's pharmacopoeia... Ugh, damn it... If a child is ten years old, then, say, how much pyramidon can I give him per dose? 0.1 or 0.15?.. I've forgotten. And if he's three?.. Only children's diseases... and nothing more... Enough of these mind-boggling emergencies! Farewell, my district!.. And why does this district keep stubbornly popping into my head tonight?..

Green light... I've settled accounts with it for life... Well, that's enough... Sleep..."

"Here's a letter. Someone brought it by chance..."

"Give it here."

A nurse was standing in my hallway. A coat with a mangy collar was thrown over her white robe with its hospital stamp. Snow was melting on the cheap blue envelope.

"Are you on duty in the admissions room tonight?" I asked, yawning.

"Yes."

"No one there?"

"No, it's empty."

"If," (a yawn was splitting my mouth, making me pronounce the words sloppily), "if they bring anyone in... let me know here... I'm going to bed..."

"Very well. May I go?"

"Yes, yes. Go."

She left. The door squeaked, and I shuffled in my slippers to the bedroom, clumsily and crookedly tearing the envelope open with my fingers along the way.

Inside was an oblong, crumpled form with the blue stamp of my district, my hospital... An unforgettable form...

I grinned.

"How interesting... I spent the whole evening thinking about the district, and now it's appeared itself to remind me... A premonition..."

Beneath the stamp, a prescription was scribbled in indelible pencil. Latin words, illegible, crossed out...

"I don't understand a thing... A muddled prescription..." I muttered, and stared at the word "morphini..." What, now, was so unusual about this prescription?.. Ah, yes... A four-percent solution! Who prescribes a four-percent solution of morphine?.. What for?!

I turned the sheet over, and my yawning stopped. On the reverse side of the sheet, in ink, in a weak and sprawling hand, was written:

"February 11, 1918.

Dear Colleague!

Forgive me for writing on a scrap. There's no paper at hand. I have fallen very seriously and badly ill. There is no one to help me, and I don't want to seek help from anyone but you.

For the second month I've been stuck in your former district; I know you are in the town and relatively not far from me.

In the name of our friendship and university years, I beg you to come to me as soon as possible. Even for a day. Even for an hour. And if you tell me I am hopeless, I will believe you... But perhaps I can be saved?.. Yes, perhaps I can still be saved?.. Will hope dawn for me? Please, don't tell anyone the contents of this letter."

"Marya! Go to the admissions room right now and call the duty nurse for me... what's her name?.. Well, I've forgotten... In short, the duty nurse who just brought me the letter. Hurry!"

"Right away."

A few minutes later the nurse stood before me, and the snow was melting on the mangy cat-fur that had served as material for her collar.

"Who brought the letter?"

"Oh, I don't know. A man with a beard. A co-operator, he said. He was on his way to town."

"Hm... well, you may go. No, wait. I'll write a note to the head physician now, take it to him, please, and bring me back the reply."

"Very well."

My note to the head physician:

"February 13, 1918.

Dear Pavel Illarionovich,

I have just received a letter from my university comrade, Dr. Polyakov. He is stationed at my former district in Gorelovo, in complete solitude. He appears to be seriously ill. I consider it my duty to go and see him. If you permit, I will hand over my department to Dr. Rodovich for one day tomorrow and go to Polyakov. The man is helpless.

Respectfully yours, Dr. Bomgard."

The head physician's reply note:

"Dear Vladimir Mikhailovich, go ahead.

Petrov."

I spent the evening poring over the railway guide. The way to get to Gorelovo was this: tomorrow, leave at two in the afternoon with the Moscow mail train, travel thirty versts by rail, get off at Station N, and from there travel twenty-two versts by sleigh to the Gorelovo hospital.

"If I'm lucky, I'll be in Gorelovo tomorrow night," I thought, lying in bed. "What has he fallen ill with? Typhus, pneumonia? Neither one nor the other... He'd have written simply: 'I've fallen ill with pneumonia.' But this is a muddled, slightly false letter... 'Fallen seriously... and badly ill...' With what? Syphilis? Yes, undoubtedly syphilis. He's in terror... he's hiding it... he's afraid... But what horses, I wonder, will I get from the station to go to Gorelovo? It'll be a bad business if I arrive at the station at twilight, and there's nothing to travel on... Well, no. I'll find a way. I'll find someone at the station who has horses. Send a telegram for him to send horses! Pointless! The telegram would arrive a day after my arrival... It won't fly through the air to Gorelovo. It will lie at the station until some chance traveller happens by. I know that Gorelovo. Oh, godforsaken place!"

The letter on the form lay on the night table in the circle of light from the lamp, and beside it stood the companion of irritable insomnia, an ashtray bristling with cigarette butts. I tossed and turned on the crumpled sheet, and vexation was born in my soul. The letter began to irritate me.

"Really: if it's nothing acute, but, say, syphilis, then why doesn't he come here himself? Why must I rush off through the blizzard to him? What, am I going to cure his lues in one evening, or what? Or cancer of the esophagus? What cancer! He's two years younger than me. He's twenty-five years old... 'Seriously...' Sarcoma? The letter is absurd, hysterical. A letter that could give the recipient a migraine... And here it is. The little vein on my temple is tightening... So, I'll wake up in the morning, and it will creep up from the vein to the crown, shackle half my head, and by evening I'll be swallowing pyramidon with caffeine. And how will that be in a sleigh with a migraine? I'll have to borrow the feldsher's travelling fur coat, I'll freeze tomorrow in my own coat... What's the matter with him?.. 'Will hope dawn...'—that's how they write in novels, not in serious doctors' letters!.. Sleep, sleep... Don't think about it anymore. Tomorrow everything will be clear... Tomorrow."

I turned off the switch, and instantly darkness devoured my room. Sleep... The little vein aches... But I have no right to be angry with a man for an absurd letter, not yet knowing what the matter is. A man suffers in his own way, and so he writes to a friend. Well, as best he can, as he understands... And it's unworthy, because of a migraine, because of the trouble, to vilify him, even mentally. Perhaps this letter isn't false or romantic after all. I haven't seen him, Seryozhka Polyakov, for two years, but I remember him perfectly. He was always a very sensible man... Yes. So some misfortune must have befallen him... And my little vein feels easier...

Sleep must be coming. What is the mechanism of sleep?.. I read about it in physiology... but it's a dark story... I don't understand what sleep means... how brain cells fall asleep?! I don't understand, I confess. But for some reason I'm sure that even the author of the physiology textbook isn't very certain either... One theory is as good as another... There stands Seryozhka Polyakov in a green tunic with gold buttons, leaning over a zinc table, and on the table is a corpse...

Hm, yes... well, this is a dream...

**III**

Knock, knock... Boom, boom, boom... Aha... Who? Who? What?.. Oh, they're knocking... oh, damn it, they're knocking... Where am I? What am I?.. What's the matter? Yes, in my own bed... Then why are they waking me? They have the right to, because I'm on duty. Wake up, Dr. Bomgard.

There's Marya shuffling to the door to open it. What time is it? Half past midnight... Night. So I've only slept one hour. The migraine? Present. Here it is!

A soft knocking continued at the door.

"What is it?"

I opened the door to the dining room a crack. The nurse's face looked at me from the darkness, and I saw immediately that it was pale, that her eyes were wide, alarmed.

"Who have they brought?"

"The doctor from the Gorelovo district," the nurse answered hoarsely and loudly, "the doctor shot himself."

"Po-lya-ko-va? It can't be! Polyakov?!"

"I don't know the name."

"Listen... Right away, I'm coming right away. And you run to the head physician, wake him, this instant. Tell him I'm urgently calling him to the admissions room."

The nurse darted off—and the white spot vanished from sight.

Two minutes later, an angry blizzard, dry and prickly, lashed my cheeks on the porch, blew up the skirts of my coat, chilled my frightened body.

In the windows of the admissions room, a white and restless light flared. On the porch, in a cloud of snow, I ran into the head surgeon, rushing to the same place I was.

"Yours? Polyakov?" he asked, clearing his throat.

"I don't understand anything. Evidently it's him," I replied, and we hurriedly entered the room.

From a bench, a muffled woman rose to meet us. Familiar eyes looked at me tearfully from under the edge of a brown shawl. I recognized Marya Vlasyevna, the midwife from Gorelovo, my faithful assistant during births in the Gorelovo hospital.

"Polyakov?" I asked.

"Yes," replied Marya Vlasyevna, "such a horror, Doctor, I travelled, trembling the whole way, praying I'd get him here in time..."

"When?"

"This morning, at dawn," Marya Vlasyevna muttered, "the watchman came running, saying... 'there's been a shot in the doctor's apartment...'"

Beneath the lamp, shedding its nasty, alarming light, lay Dr. Polyakov, and from the first glance at his lifeless, seemingly stone-like feet in their felt boots, my heart gave its habitual, anxious lurch.

They removed his cap—and his matted, damp hair was revealed. My hands, the nurse's hands, Marya Vlasyevna's hands flickered over Polyakov, and white gauze with spreading yellow-red spots emerged from under his coat. His chest rose weakly. I felt his pulse and faltered; the pulse was disappearing under my fingers, stretching out and breaking off into a thread with knots—frequent and fragile. The surgeon's hand was already reaching for his shoulder, pinching the pale body to inject camphor. At that moment, the wounded man parted his lips, upon which a pinkish bloody streak appeared, barely moved his blue lips, and dryly, weakly articulated:

"Stop the camphor. To hell with it."

"Be quiet," the surgeon answered him and pushed the yellow oil under his skin.

"The pericardial sac, I suppose, is touched," whispered Marya Vlasyevna, gripping the edge of the table tenaciously and beginning to gaze intently at the wounded man's endless eyelids (his eyes were closed). Grey-violet shadows, like sunset shadows, began to bloom ever brighter in the hollows by the wings of his nose, and a fine, almost mercurial sweat, like dew, stood out on the shadows.

"Revolver?" the surgeon asked, his cheek twitching.

"A Browning," Marya Vlasyevna faltered.

"E-ekh," the surgeon suddenly said, as if with spite and vexation, and suddenly, waving his hand, stepped away.

I turned to him in fright, not understanding. Someone else's eyes flickered over his shoulder. Another doctor had arrived.

Polyakov suddenly moved his mouth, crookedly, like a sleepy person trying to brush away a clinging fly, and then his lower jaw began to move, as if he were choking on a little lump and trying to swallow it. Ah, those who have seen nasty revolver or gunshot wounds know this movement well!

Marya Vlasyevna winced painfully, sighed.

"Dr. Bomgard," Polyakov said, barely audibly.

"I'm here," I whispered, and my voice sounded tender right by his lips.

"The notebook... for you..." Polyakov responded hoarsely and even more weakly.

Then he opened his eyes and raised them to the cheerless ceiling receding into darkness. His dark pupils seemed to fill with light from within, the whites of his eyes became as if transparent, bluish. The eyes paused in the heights, then grew dim and lost that fleeting beauty.

Dr. Polyakov died.

__________

Night. Near dawn. The lamp burns very brightly, because the little town is asleep and there is plenty of electric current. Everything is silent, and Polyakov's body is in the chapel. Night.

On the table, before eyes inflamed from reading, lie an opened envelope and a sheet of paper. On it is written:

"Dear Comrade!

I shall not wait for you. I have changed my mind about seeking treatment. It is hopeless. And I do not want to suffer any longer. I have had enough of a taste. I warn others: be careful with the white crystals, soluble in 25 parts of water. I trusted them too much, and they have destroyed me. I give you my diary as a gift. You always seemed to me an inquisitive man and a lover of human documents. If it interests you, read the history of my illness.

Farewell. Yours, S. Polyakov."

A postscript in large letters:

"I ask that no one be blamed for my death.

Physician Sergey Polyakov.

February 13, 1918."

Beside the suicide's letter lies a notebook, the kind of general notebook with a black oilcloth cover. The first half of the pages have been torn out of it. In the remaining half are brief notes, at the beginning in pencil or ink, in a clear, small hand; at the end of the notebook, in indelible pencil and in thick red pencil, in a careless hand, a skipping hand, with many abbreviated words.

**IV**

"...year, January 20th.

...and very glad. And thank God: the more remote, the better. I can't stand seeing people, and here I won't see any people except sick peasants. But they surely won't touch my wound? Others, however, no worse than me, have been scattered among district zemstvos. My whole graduation class, not subject to conscription (militiamen of the second category, class of 1916), were placed in zemstvos. However, this is of no interest to anyone. Among my friends, I've only found out about Ivanov and Bomgard. Ivanov chose Arkhangelsk province (a matter of taste), and Bomgard, as a feldsheritsa said, is stuck in a remote district like mine, three districts away from me, in Gorelovo. I thought of writing to him, but changed my mind. I don't wish to see or hear people.

January 21st.

Blizzard. It's nothing.

January 25th.

What a clear sunset. Migrenin—a compound of antipyrin, coffein, and ac. citric. In powders of 1.0... is 1.0 possible?.. It is.

February 3rd.

Today I got last week's newspapers. I didn't start reading, but I was still drawn to look at the theatre section. 'Aida' was on last week. So she went out onto the platform and sang: 'My dear friend, come to me...'

Her voice is extraordinary, and how strange that a clear, huge voice was given to such a dark little soul...

(Here a break; two or three pages are torn out.)

...of course, it's unworthy, Dr. Polyakov. And it's stupidly schoolboyish to assail a woman with foul language because she left! She didn't want to live with me—she left. That's the end. How simple it all is, really. An opera singer got together with a young doctor, lived with him for a year, and left.

Kill her? Kill her! Oh, how stupid it all is, empty. Hopeless!

I don't want to think. I don't want to...

February 11th.

Still blizzards, always blizzards... Burying me! Whole evenings I am alone, alone. I light the lamp and sit. During the day I still see people. But I work mechanically. I've grown accustomed to the work. It's not as terrible as I thought earlier. However, the wartime hospital helped me a lot. At least I didn't arrive here completely illiterate.

Today I performed a version operation for the first time.

So, three people are buried here under the snow: me, Anna Kirillovna—the feldsheritsa-midwife, and the feldsher. The feldsher is married. They (the feldsher staff) live in the annex. And I am alone.

February 15th.

Last night an interesting thing happened. I was getting ready for bed when suddenly I got pains in the stomach region. But what pains! Cold sweat broke out on my forehead. Still, our medicine is a dubious science, I must note. Why would a man with absolutely no disease of the stomach or intestines (appendicitis, for example), with an excellent liver and kidneys, whose intestines function completely normally, have such pains at night that he rolls around in bed?

Groaning, I made it to the kitchen, where the cook and her husband, Vlas, sleep. I sent Vlas to Anna Kirillovna. She came to me during the night and had to inject me with morphine. She says I was completely green. Why?

I don't like our feldsher. He's unsociable, but Anna Kirillovna is a very nice and developed person. I'm surprised how a not-young woman can live in complete solitude in this snowy coffin. Her husband is a prisoner of war in Germany.

I cannot help but praise the one who first extracted morphine from poppy heads. A true benefactor of humanity. The pains stopped seven minutes after the injection. Interesting: the pains came in full waves, allowing no pauses, so I positively suffocated, as if a red-hot crowbar had been thrust into my stomach and twisted. About four minutes after the injection, I began to distinguish the wave-like nature of the pain:

It would be very good if a doctor had the opportunity to test many medicines on himself. He would have a completely different understanding of their action. After the injection, for the first time in recent months, I slept deeply and well—without thoughts of the one who deceived me.

February 16th.

Today Anna Kirillovna, during office hours, inquired how I was feeling, and said that for the first time in all this time she saw me not gloomy.

"Am I gloomy?"

"Very," she answered convincingly, and added that she was amazed that I was always silent.

"I'm just that kind of person."

But that's a lie. I was a very cheerful person before my family drama.

Twilight comes early. I am alone in the apartment. In the evening, pain came, but not strong, like a shadow of yesterday's pain, somewhere behind the breastbone. Fearing a return of yesterday's attack, I injected myself in the thigh with one centigram.

The pain stopped almost instantly. It's good that Anna Kirillovna left the vial.

18th.

Four injections are not terrible.

February 25th.

What an odd woman this Anna Kirillovna is! As if I'm not a doctor; 1/2 syringe = 0.015 morph.? Yes.

March 1st.

Dr. Polyakov, be careful. Nonsense.

Twilight.

But now it's been half a month since I've returned in thought to the woman who deceived me. The motif from her part, Amneris, has left me. I am very proud of this. I am a man.

Anna K. has become my secret wife. It couldn't have been any other way. We are confined on a desert island.

The snow has changed, become somehow greyer. There are no more severe frosts, but blizzards still start up from time to time...

March 3rd.

I must describe the action of morphine. Subcutaneous injection of 0.015 (one syringe of a two-percent solution). The first minute: a sensation of touching the neck. This touch becomes warm and expands. In the second minute, a cold wave suddenly passes through the pit of the stomach, and following this, an extraordinary clarity of thought and a burst of working capacity begin. Absolutely all unpleasant sensations cease. This is the highest point of manifestation of a person's spiritual strength. And if I weren't spoiled by my medical education, I would say that a person can only work normally after an injection of morphine. Really: what the hell good is a person if the slightest neuralgia can completely knock him out of the saddle!

Anna K. is afraid. I reassured her, saying that since childhood I had been distinguished by a tremendous willpower.

March 4th.

Rumors of something grandiose. They say Nicholas II has been overthrown.

I go to bed very early. Around nine o'clock. And I sleep sweetly.

March 10th.

A revolution is taking place over there. The days are getting longer, and the twilight seems a little bluer. I have never seen such dreams before, at dawn. These are double dreams.

Moreover, the main one of them, I would say, is transparent.

So: I see a frightfully lit-up ramp, from which a multi-colored ribbon of lights blazes. Amneris, shaking a green feather, sings. The orchestra, completely unearthly, is extraordinarily full-toned. However, I cannot convey this in words. In short, in a normal dream, music is soundless... (in a normal dream? It's still a question which dream is more normal! However, I'm joking...) it's soundless, but in my dream it is heard completely divinely. And the main thing is that I can, of my own will, increase or decrease the music. I remember, in 'War and Peace' it is described how Petya Rostov, half-asleep, experienced the same state. Leo Tolstoy is a remarkable writer!

Now about transparency: so, through the shimmering colors of 'Aida,' the edge of my desk, visible from the study door, appears completely real, the lamp, the glossy floor, and, breaking through the wave of the Bolshoi Theatre orchestra, clear footsteps are heard, treading pleasantly like muted castanets.

So, it is eight o'clock—that is Anna K., coming to wake me and tell me what's going on in the waiting room.

She doesn't realize that there's no need to wake me, that I hear everything and can talk with her.

And I performed such an experiment yesterday:

Anna: Sergey Vasilievich...

I: I hear you... (quietly to the music—'louder').

Music—a great chord. D-sharp...

Anna: Twenty people have registered.

Amneris (sings).

However, this cannot be conveyed on paper.

Are these dreams harmful? Oh no. After them, I rise strong and alert. And I work well. I even developed an interest, which I didn't have before. And no wonder, all my thoughts were concentrated on my former wife.

But now I am calm.

I am calm.

March 19th.

At night I had a quarrel with Anna K.

"I won't prepare the solution anymore."

I began to persuade her:

"Nonsense, Annusya. What am I, a child or something?"

"I won't. You'll perish."

"Well, as you wish. Understand, I have pains in my chest!"

"Get treatment."

"Where?"

"Go on leave. Morphine is not treatment." (Then she thought and added.) "I cannot forgive myself for preparing that second vial for you then."

"What, am I a morphinist, or what?"

"Yes, you are becoming a morphinist."

"So you won't go?"

"No."

At that moment, I discovered in myself for the first time an unpleasant ability to get angry and, most importantly, to shout at people when I am in the wrong.

However, it wasn't immediate. I went into the bedroom. I looked. There was barely a splash left in the bottom of the vial. I drew it into the syringe—it turned out to be a quarter of a syringe. I threw the syringe, nearly breaking it, and trembled myself. I picked it up carefully, examined it—not a single crack. I sat in the bedroom for about twenty minutes. I go out—she's gone.

She left.

Imagine—I couldn't stand it, I went to her. I knocked on the lit window of her annex. She came out, wrapped in a shawl, onto the little porch. The night was quiet, very quiet. The snow was soft. Somewhere far away in the sky, spring was in the air.

"Anna Kirillovna, would you be so kind as to give me the keys to the pharmacy."

She whispered:

"I won't give them."

"Comrade, please give me the keys to the pharmacy. I'm telling you this as a doctor."

I see in the twilight, her face changed, became very pale, and her eyes deepened, sank, turned black. And she answered in a voice that stirred pity in my soul.

But then anger surged over me again.

She said:

"Why, why do you speak like that? Oh, Sergey Vasilievich, I am—pitying you."

And here she freed her hands from under the shawl, and I see that the keys are in her hands. So she had come out to me and brought them.

I (rudely):

"Give me the keys!"

And I snatched them from her hands.

And I went towards the whitening hospital building along the rotten, bouncing plank walkway.

Rage hissed in my soul, and primarily because I hadn't the slightest idea how to prepare a morphine solution for subcutaneous injection. I'm a doctor, not a feldsheritsa!

I walked and trembled.

And I hear, behind me, like a faithful dog, she followed. And tenderness welled up in me, but I stifled it. I turned around and, baring my teeth, said:

"Will you do it or not?"

And she waved her hand, like one doomed, "it's all the same, I suppose," and quietly answered:

"Come on, I'll do it..."

An hour later I was in a normal state. Of course, I apologized to her for the senseless rudeness. I don't know myself how it happened with me. Before, I was a polite person.

She reacted strangely to my apology. She sank to her knees, pressed herself to my hands and said:

"I'm not angry with you. No. I know now that you are lost. I know it already. And I curse myself for having given you that injection then."

I reassured her as best I could, assuring her that she was absolutely not to blame, that I myself was responsible for my actions. I promised her that from the next day I would begin seriously to wean myself off it, reducing the dose.

"How much did you just inject?"

"Nonsense. Three syringes of a one-percent solution."

She clasped her head and fell silent.

"Don't worry!"

Essentially speaking, her worry is understandable to me. Indeed, morphium hydrochloricum is a formidable thing. Habituation to it develops very quickly. But a small habit surely isn't morphinism?..

...To tell the truth, this woman is my only true, real person. And, essentially, she should be my wife. I've forgotten that other one. Forgotten. And still, thank morphine for that...

April 8, 1917.

This is torment.

April 9th.

Spring is terrible.

The devil in the vial. Cocaine—the devil in a vial!

Its effect is this:

Upon injecting one syringe of a two-percent solution, a state of calm sets in almost instantly, immediately turning into ecstasy and bliss. And this lasts only one or two minutes. And then everything disappears without a trace, as if it never was. Pain, horror, darkness set in. Spring is thundering, black birds flit from bare branch to branch, and in the distance the forest, a bristly, broken black line, stretches towards the sky, and behind it burns the first spring sunset, covering a quarter of the sky.

I measure with steps the lonely, empty large room in my doctor's apartment, diagonally.

April 13th.

I, the unfortunate Dr. Polyakov, who fell ill with morphinism in February of this year, warn all who may suffer the same fate as me: do not try to replace morphine with cocaine. Cocaine is the nastiest and most treacherous poison. Yesterday Anna barely brought me round with camphor, and today I am half a corpse...

May 6, 1917.

I haven't picked up my diary for quite a while. That's a shame. In essence, this is not a diary, but a case history, and I obviously have a professional gravitation towards my only friend in the world (if one doesn't count my sorrowful and often tearful friend Anna).

So, if we're keeping a case history, then this is it: I inject myself with morphine twice a day: at 5 p.m. (after dinner) and at 12 p.m. before sleep.

The solution is three-percent, two syringes. Consequently, I get 0.06 at one time.

Quite a lot!

My previous notes are somewhat hysterical. There's nothing particularly terrible. My work capacity isn't affected at all. On the contrary, I live the whole day on the previous night's injection. I cope with operations splendidly, I am impeccably attentive to prescriptions, and I vouch on my medical word that my morphinism has done no harm to my patients. I hope it won't in the future either. But something else torments me. I keep feeling that someone will find out about my vice. And it's hard for me, during office hours, to feel on my back the heavy, searching gaze of my assistant, the feldsher.

Nonsense! He doesn't suspect. Nothing gives me away. My pupils could betray me only in the evening, and in the evening I never encounter him.

The terrible depletion of morphine in our pharmacy I made up for by going to the district center. But even there I had to endure unpleasant moments. The warehouse manager took my requisition, into which I had prudently also written all sorts of other nonsense, like caffeine (of which we have plenty), and said:

"40 grams of morphine?"

And I felt myself hiding my eyes like a schoolboy. I felt myself blushing...

He said:

"We don't have that much. I'll give you about ten grams."

And indeed, he didn't have it, but it seemed to me that he had penetrated my secret, that he was probing and boring into me with his eyes, and I became agitated and tormented.

No, only the pupils, only the pupils are dangerous, and therefore I will make it a rule: not to encounter people in the evening. However, it would be hard to find a more convenient place than my district for this; for more than six months now I have seen no one except my patients. And they have absolutely no concern with me.

May 18th.

Sultry night. There will be a thunderstorm. A black belly is growing and swelling in the distance beyond the forest. There, it flashed, pale and alarming. A thunderstorm is coming.

A book is before my eyes, and in it is written regarding abstinence from morphine:

"...great anxiety, a state of alarming melancholy, irritability, weakening of memory, sometimes hallucinations and a slight degree of clouding of consciousness..."

I have not experienced hallucinations, but regarding the rest I can say: oh, what dull, official, meaningless words!

"A state of alarming melancholy!"..

No, I, who have fallen ill with this terrible disease, warn doctors to be more compassionate with their patients. It is not an "alarming melancholy," but a slow death that overtakes the morphinist, the moment you deprive him of morphine for an hour or two. The air is unsatisfying, you cannot swallow it... there is not a cell in the body that does not yearn... For what? This cannot be defined or explained. In a word, the person is gone. He is switched off. A corpse moves, yearns, suffers. He wants nothing, thinks of nothing but morphine. Morphine!

Death from thirst is heavenly, blissful death compared to the thirst for morphine. Thus a person buried alive probably catches the last insignificant bubbles of air in the coffin and tears the skin on his chest with his fingernails. Thus a heretic at the stake groans and stirs when the first tongues of flame lick his feet...

Death—a dry, slow death...

That is what is hidden under those professorial words "alarming melancholy."

I can't take it anymore. And so I took it and injected myself just now. A breath. Another breath.

Easier. And now... now... a minty coolness in the pit of the stomach...

Three syringes of a three-percent solution. That will last me until midnight...

Nonsense. This note is nonsense. It's not so terrible. Sooner or later I'll stop!.. But now, sleep, sleep.

With this foolish struggle against morphine, I only torment and weaken myself.

(Further on, about twenty pages have been cut out of the notebook.)

...rya

...to vomit at 4:30.

When I feel better, I will record my terrible impressions.

November 14, 1917

So, after escaping from the clinic in Moscow from Dr... (the surname is carefully crossed out) I am home again. The rain is falling like a veil and hiding the world from me. And let it hide him from me. I don't need him, just as no one in the world needs me. The shooting and the upheaval I experienced while still in the clinic. But the thought of quitting this treatment furtively matured in me even before the fighting in the streets of Moscow. Thank morphine for making me brave. No shooting scares me. And indeed, what can frighten a person who thinks only of one thing—of the wondrous, divine crystals. When the feldsheritsa, completely terrorized by the cannon booming...

(Here a page is torn out.)

...tore out this page so that no one would read the shameful description of how a man with a diploma fled furtively and cowardly and stole his own suit.

The suit, indeed!

I even took a hospital shirt. There was no time for that. The next day, having had an injection, I revived and returned to Dr. N. He met me with pity, but through this pity there still showed contempt. And this was unjust. After all, he is a psychiatrist and should understand that I am not always in control of myself. I am sick. Why despise me? I returned the hospital shirt.

He said:

"Thank you," and added: "Well, what do you think of doing now?"

I said briskly (I was in a state of euphoria at that moment):

"I've decided to return to my remote place, especially since my leave has expired. I am very grateful to you for your help, I feel significantly better. I will continue treatment there on my own."

He answered thus:

"You don't feel better at all. It's really funny to me that you say that to me. One glance at your pupils is enough. Well, who are you telling this to?.."

"Professor, I can't break the habit immediately... especially now with all these events happening... the shooting has completely frayed my nerves..."

"It's over. Here's the new government. Admit yourself again."

Then I remembered everything... the cold corridors... the empty walls painted with oil paint... and me crawling like a dog with a broken leg... waiting for something... What? A hot bath?... A tiny injection of 0.005 morphine. A dose that, true, you won't die from... but still... all the anguish remains, lies like a burden, as it did before... The empty nights, the shirt I tore on myself, begging to be let out?..

No. No. You invented morphine, extracted it from the dry, clicking heads of a divine plant, well then find a way to treat it without suffering! I shook my head stubbornly. Then he stirred, and I suddenly rushed in fright to the door. It seemed to me that he wanted to lock the door behind me and forcibly keep me in the clinic...

The professor turned purple.

"I am not a prison warden," he said not without irritation, "and this is not Butyrki. Sit still. You boasted that you were completely normal two weeks ago. And yet..." he expressively repeated my gesture of fright, "I'm not detaining you, sir."

"Professor, give me back my signed statement. I implore you," and even my voice trembled pitifully.

"Certainly."

He clicked his key in the desk and gave me back my signed statement (to the effect that I undertook to complete the entire two-month course of treatment and that they could detain me in the clinic, etc., in short, the usual type).

With a trembling hand I took the note and hid it, stammering:

"Thank you."

Then I got up to leave. And I went.

"Dr. Polyakov!" came the call after me. I turned around, holding the door handle. "Look here," he began, "think it over. Understand that you'll end up in a psychiatric clinic anyway, well, a little later... And moreover you'll arrive in a much worse condition. I still considered you as a doctor. But then you'll come already in a state of complete mental breakdown. You, my dear fellow, essentially shouldn't be practicing, and it would probably be criminal not to warn your place of work."

I shuddered and clearly felt the color drain from my face (though I have very little of it anyway).

"I," I said hoarsely, "implore you, Professor, don't tell anyone anything... What, they'll remove me from my post... They'll brand me as sick... Why do you want to do this to me?"

"Go," he shouted vexedly, "go. I won't say anything. They'll send you back anyway..."

I left and, I swear, I twitched with pain and shame the whole way... Why?...

It's very simple. Oh, my friend, my faithful diary. You won't betray me, will you? It's not about the suit, but that I stole morphine in the clinic. 3 cubic centimeters in crystals and 10 grams of a one-percent solution.

It's not only this that interests me, but also this: the key was in the cupboard. Well, what if it hadn't been? Would I have broken into the cupboard or not? Honestly?

I would have broken in.

So, Dr. Polyakov is a thief. I'll manage to tear out the page.

Well, about practicing, he overdid it after all. Yes, I am a degenerate. Absolutely correct. The disintegration of my moral personality has begun. But I can work; I cannot cause any evil or harm to any of my patients.

Yes, why did I steal? Very simple. I decided that during the fighting and all the turmoil associated with the upheaval, I wouldn't be able to get morphine anywhere. But when it quieted down, I got another 15 grams of a one-percent solution in a pharmacy on the outskirts—a useless and tedious thing for me (I'd have to inject nine syringes). And I still had to humiliate myself. The pharmacist demanded a stamp, looked at me gloomily and suspiciously. But the next day, having come back to normal, I got 20 grams in crystals without any delay at another pharmacy—I wrote a prescription for the hospital (along with it, of course, I prescribed caffeine and aspirin). And after all, why should I have to hide, be afraid? Really, is it written on my forehead that I'm a morphinist? In the end, whose business is it?

And is the disintegration so great? I call these notes as witnesses. They are fragmentary, but I'm not a writer after all! Are there any insane thoughts in them? In my opinion, I reason completely sanely.

A morphinist has one happiness that no one can take away from him—the ability to spend life in complete solitude. And solitude means important, significant thoughts, contemplation, calm, wisdom...

Night flows on, black and silent. Somewhere a bare forest, beyond it a river, cold, autumn. Far, far away, ruffled, riotous Moscow. I don't care about anything, I need nothing, and I am drawn nowhere.

Burn, fire, in my lamp, burn quietly; I want to rest after my Moscow adventures, I want to forget them.

And I forgot.

Forgot.

November 18th.

Light frosts. It's dried up. I went out for a walk to the river along a path, because I almost never breathe fresh air.

Disintegration of personality—disintegration, but still I make attempts to abstain from it. For example, this morning I didn't inject myself (now I inject three times a day, three syringes of a four-percent solution). It's inconvenient. I feel sorry for Anna. Each new percentage kills her. I feel sorry. Oh, what a person!

Yes... So... well... when it got bad, I decided to suffer a little longer anyway (Professor N should have seen me then) and postpone the injection, and I went off to the river.

What a wilderness. Not a sound, not a rustle. Twilight is not yet here, but it is lurking somewhere and creeping through the marshes, over the hummocks, among the stumps... It comes, comes towards the Levkovo hospital... And I crawl, leaning on a stick (to tell the truth, I've weakened somewhat lately).

And then I see, from the river along the slope, rushing towards me quickly, and not moving her little legs under her brightly colored bell-shaped skirt, an old woman with yellow hair... At first I didn't understand her and wasn't even scared. An old woman like any old woman. Strange—why is the old woman bare-headed in the cold, in just a blouse?.. And then: where did this old woman come from? What old woman? Our consultations in Levkovo finish, the last peasant sleighs disperse, and for ten versts around—there's no one. Fogs, marshes, forests! And then suddenly a cold sweat ran down my spine—I understood! The old woman isn't running, but flying, not touching the ground. Good, eh? But it wasn't this that tore a cry from me, but that in the old woman's hands—were pitchforks. Why was I so frightened? Why? I fell onto one knee, stretching out my arms, shielding myself, so as not to see her, then I turned and, hobbling, ran towards the house as if to a place of salvation, wanting nothing except that my heart shouldn't burst, that I should quickly run into the warm rooms, see the living Anna... and morphine...

And I ran there.

__________

Nonsense. An empty hallucination. A chance hallucination.

November 19th.

Vomiting. This is bad.

My nightly conversation with Anna on the 21st:

Anna: The feldsher knows.

I: Really? It doesn't matter. Trifles.

Anna: If you don't leave here for the town, I'll hang myself. Do you hear? Look at your hands, look.

I: They tremble a little. That doesn't prevent me from working at all.

Anna: Look—they're transparent. Just bone and skin... Look at your face... Listen, Seryozha. Leave, I implore you, leave...

I: And you?

Anna: Leave. Leave. You are perishing.

I: Well, that's putting it strongly. But I really don't understand myself why I've weakened so quickly? It's not even a full year since I've been ill. Evidently, I have such a constitution.

Anna (sadly): What can bring you back to life? Perhaps that Amneris of yours—your wife?

I: Oh no. Calm down. Thank morphine, it freed me from her. Instead of her—morphine.

Anna: Oh my God... what am I to do?..

__________

I thought that women like this Anna existed only in novels. And if I ever recover, I will unite my fate with hers forever. Let that other man not return from Germany.

December 27th.

I haven't picked up this notebook for a long time. I am wrapped up, the horses are waiting. Bomgard has left the Gorelovo district, and I have been sent to replace him. In my district—a woman doctor.

Anna is here... She will come to visit me...

Even if it's thirty versts.

__________

We decided firmly that from January 1st I will take leave for one month due to illness and go to the professor in Moscow. Again I'll give a signed undertaking, and for a month I will suffer inhuman torment in his clinic.

Farewell, Levkovo. Anna, until we meet again.

January.

I didn't go. I cannot part with my crystalline soluble little god.

During treatment I will perish.

And more and more often the thought comes to me that I don't need treatment.

January 15th.

Vomiting in the morning.

Three syringes of a four-percent solution at twilight.

Three syringes of a four-percent solution at night.

January 16th.

Operation day, therefore great abstinence—from night until 6 p.m.

At twilight—the most terrible time—already in the apartment I distinctly heard a voice, monotonous and threatening, which repeated:

"Sergey Vasilievich. Sergey Vasilievich."

After the injection, everything passed immediately.

January 17th.

Blizzard—no consultations. Read a psychiatry textbook during abstinence, and it made a terrifying impression on me. I am lost, there is no hope.

I am afraid of rustles, people are hateful to me during abstinence. I am afraid of them. During euphoria, I love them all, but prefer solitude.

__________

Here I need to be careful—here there is a feldsher and two midwives. I need to be very attentive so as not to give myself away. I have become experienced and won't give myself away. No one will know, as long as I have a supply of morphine. I prepare the solutions myself or send Anna a prescription in advance. Once she made an attempt (absurd) to substitute a two-percent solution for a five-percent one. She brought it herself from Levkovo in the cold and snowstorm. And because of that we had a terrible quarrel at night. I convinced her not to do it. I told the staff here that I am ill. I racked my brains for a long time over what kind of illness to invent. I told them I have rheumatism in my legs and severe neurasthenia. They have been warned that I am leaving in February on leave to Moscow for treatment. Things are going smoothly. No disruptions in work. I avoid operating on days when I have uncontrollable vomiting with hiccups. Therefore I had to add a gastric catarrh. Ah, too many illnesses for one person!

The staff here are compassionate and are themselves urging me to go on leave.

__________

Appearance: thin, pale with a waxy pallor.

I took a bath and weighed myself on the hospital scales. Last year I weighed 4 poods, now 3 poods 15 pounds. I was frightened when I looked at the needle, then it passed.

On my forearms, incessant boils, the same on my thighs. I don't know how to prepare solutions under sterile conditions; besides, about three times I injected with an unboiled syringe, being in a great hurry before a trip.

This is unacceptable.

January 18th.

I had this hallucination:

I am waiting for some pale people to appear in the black windows. This is unbearable. I have only one curtain. I took some gauze from the hospital and covered the window. I couldn't think of a pretext.

Ah, damn it! Why, in the end, must I invent a pretext for every action of mine? Really, this is torment, not life.

Do I express my thoughts smoothly? In my opinion, smoothly. Life? Ridiculous!

January 19th.

Today during a break in consultations, when we were resting and smoking in the pharmacy, the feldsher, while mixing powders, told (for some reason with a laugh) how one feldsheritsa, suffering from morphinism and unable to get morphine, would take half a wine glass of opium tincture. I didn't know where to look during this painful story. What's funny about it? I hate him. What's funny about this? What?

I left the pharmacy with a thief's gait.

"What do you find funny about this disease?.."

But I restrained myself, restrain...

In my position, one shouldn't be particularly arrogant with people.

Ah, feldsher. He is as cruel as those psychiatrists who cannot do anything, anything, anything to help a patient.

Nothing.

The preceding lines were written during abstinence, and there is much injustice in them. Now it is a moonlit night. I lie here after a bout of vomiting, weak. I cannot raise my hand high and I scribble my thoughts with a pencil. They are pure and proud. I am happy for a few hours. Ahead of me is sleep. Above me is the moon, and on it a corona. Nothing is frightening after an injection.

February 1st.

Anna arrived. She is yellow, ill.

I've finished her off. Finished. Yes, on my conscience there is a great sin.

I gave her my oath that I will leave in mid-February.

Will I keep it?

Yes. I will. If I am alive.

February 3rd.

So: an icy hill. Icy and endless, like the one from which, in a fairy tale, the sleigh carried away little Kay. My last flight down this hill, and I know what awaits me at the bottom. Ah, Anna, great sorrow will soon be yours, if you loved me...

February 11th.

I have decided this. I will turn to Bomgard. Why precisely to him? Because he is not a psychiatrist, because he is young and a university comrade. He is healthy, strong, but gentle, if I am right. I remember him. Perhaps he will... I will find sympathy in him. He will think of something. Let him take me to Moscow. I cannot go to him. I have already received my leave. I am lying down. I don't go to the hospital.

I slandered the feldsher. Well, he laughed... It doesn't matter. He came to visit me. Offered to listen to my chest.

I didn't allow it. Again, pretexts for refusal? I don't want to invent a pretext.

The note to Bomgard has been sent.

People! Will anyone help me? I have begun to exclaim pathetically. And if anyone read this, they would think it false. But no one will read it.

Before writing to Bomgard, I kept remembering. Especially vivid was the station in Moscow in November, when I was fleeing Moscow. What a terrible evening. I injected the stolen morphine in the toilet... This torment. They were banging on the door, voices clanged like iron, cursing me for taking so long in the place, and my hands jumped, and the hook jumped, the door would burst open any minute...

I've had boils ever since.

I cried at night, remembering this.

12th, night.

And again crying. What is this weakness and vileness at night.

1918, February 13th, at dawn in Gorelovka.

I can congratulate myself: I have been without an injection for fourteen hours! Fourteen! This is an unthinkable number. Dawn is breaking, murky and whitish. Soon I will be completely well.

Upon mature reflection: I don't need Bomgard, and I don't need anyone. It would be shameful to prolong my life even for a minute. Such a life—no, it cannot be. I have the medicine at hand. Why didn't I think of it before?

Well, let us begin. I owe nothing to anyone. I have destroyed only myself. And Anna. What can I do?

Time heals, as Amneris sang. For her, of course, it's simple and easy.

The notebook for Bomgard. That's all..."

**V**

At dawn on February 14th, 1918, in a distant little town, I read these notes of Sergey Polyakov. And here they are in full, without any changes whatsoever. I am not a psychiatrist, I cannot say with certainty whether they are instructive, whether they are needed. In my opinion, they are needed.

Now, ten years later, the pity and fear evoked by the notes have, of course, gone. That is natural, but, having re-read these notes now, when Polyakov's body has long since decayed and the memory of him has completely disappeared, I have retained an interest in them. Perhaps they are needed? I take the liberty of deciding this in the affirmative. Anna K. died in 1922 of typhus, in the same district where she worked. Amneris—Polyakov's first wife—is abroad. And she will not return.

May I publish the notes given to me?

I may. I am publishing them. Dr. Bomgard.

Autumn 1927.
