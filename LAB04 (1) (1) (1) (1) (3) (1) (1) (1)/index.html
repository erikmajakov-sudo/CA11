Zhmurki (Russian: *Жмурки*) is a 2005 Russian black comedy crime film directed by Aleksei Balabanov. Set in the chaotic 1990s, the film follows two small-time gangsters, Seryoga and Simon, who work for a local crime boss in Nizhny Novgorod. After botching a drug deal, they are sent on a series of increasingly absurd and violent errands to clean up their mess. What unfolds is a mix of dark humor, brutal violence, and sharp satire about the lawlessness and moral vacuum of post-Soviet Russia. Balabanov blends slapstick-style comedy with graphic criminal underworld brutality, turning the gangster genre into a grotesque parody of the “wild 90s.” The title *Zhmurki* (a children’s game similar to blind man’s bluff) serves as an ironic metaphor for the blind greed, chaos, and deadly games of the era.

Brother (1997)

Alexei Balabanov

Short Description:

__________________________________

Newly discharged from the army, Danila goes to his older brother - a gangster and a killer - to start a new life in St. Petersburg where he's soon situated in the criminal world and asked to kill someone. There his new life starts. He meets new people, and becomes a big fan of the rock band "Nautilus Pompillius," but now he is a killer, and realizing killing is easier for him than living makes him understand he is going the wrong way. Unbeknown to their mother, Viktor is an accomplished hitman who goes by the nickname "Tatar", working for a St. Petersburg mafia boss called "Krugly". His latest target is "the Chechen", a Chechen mafia boss who had seized a local market. Krugly, who is unhappy with the amount of money that Viktor has demanded for the hit citing the risk to his own life, orders his thugs to watch the Chechen, so that he can kill the Tatar to avoid paying him.

Morphine (2008)

Alexei Balabanov

Short Description:

__________________________________

Directed by Aleksei Balabanov, Morphine (2008) is a haunting, visceral descent into both personal and societal decay. Set in the frozen isolation of 1917 Russia, the film follows Dr. Mikhail Polyakov, a fresh-faced medical graduate assigned to a remote provincial hospital. Amidst the chaos of the brewing Bolshevik Revolution, Polyakov’s idealism quickly dissolves under the weight of gruesome surgeries and professional anxiety. The narrative shifts when a routine medical mishap leads Polyakov to inject morphine for relief. What begins as a temporary escape from the brutality of rural practice spirals into a devastating addiction. Balabanov masterfully juxtaposes the doctor’s internal disintegration with the collapse of the Russian Empire; as the world outside burns with revolutionary fervor, Polyakov’s world shrinks to the tip of a needle.

War (2002)

Alexei Balabanov

Short Description:

__________________________________

War is a gritty, semi-documentary-style drama directed by Alexei Balabanov, which is set during the events of the Second Chechen War. The film follows Ivan Yermakov, a Russian conscript who is captured by Chechen fighters along with a British actor, John Boyle, and his fiancée, Margaret. The captives are held for ransom in a remote mountain village. Ivan is eventually released because he is an ordinary soldier with no value for exchange, while John is freed so he can gather money to pay for Margaret’s release. Back in England, John struggles to convince authorities and media to help. Frustrated by indifference and bureaucracy, he returns to Chechnya with Ivan to rescue Margaret themselves. What follows is a brutal journey through a lawless war zone, where survival depends on instinct, loyalty, and moral compromise.

Castle (1994)

This is blog post one

Short Description:

__________________________________

The Castle is a bleak and stylized adaptation of The Castle, directed by Alexei Balabanov. Based on Kafka’s unfinished novel, the film follows a land surveyor known only as K., who arrives in a snow-covered village governed by a mysterious and inaccessible castle. He claims to have been summoned by the authorities, but no one can confirm his status, and his attempts to gain recognition spiral into an absurd bureaucratic nightmare. The villagers live in passive submission to the castle’s distant officials, whose decisions are arbitrary and rarely explained. K. struggles to secure an audience with the elusive authorities, encountering endless delays, contradictory messages, and strange social rituals. His growing frustration exposes the irrational logic of a system where power is invisible yet absolute.
