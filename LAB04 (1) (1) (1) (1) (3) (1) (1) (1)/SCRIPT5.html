It was late in the evening when K. arrived. The village was deep in snow. The Castle hill was hidden, veiled in mist and darkness, nor was there even a glimmer of light to show that a castle was there.

On the wooden bridge leading from the main road to the village K. stood for a long time gazing into the illusory emptiness above him. Then he went on to find quarters for the night.

The inn was still awake, and although the landlord could not provide a room and was upset by such a late and unexpected arrival, he was willing to let K. sleep on a bag of straw in the parlour. K. accepted the offer.

But very shortly he was awakened. A young man dressed like a townsman, with the face of an actor, his eyes narrow and his eyebrows strongly marked, was standing beside him along with the landlord. The young man introduced himself as the son of the Castellan, and said: "This village belongs to the Castle, and whoever lives here or passes the night here does so in a manner of speaking in the Castle itself. Nobody may do that without the Count's permission. But you have no such permit, or at least you have produced none."

K. had half raised himself and said: "What village is this I have wandered into? Is there a castle here?" "Most certainly," replied the young man slowly.

"Well, then, I'll have to go and get one," said K. yawning and pushing his blanket away as if to rise up. "A permit from the Count in the middle of the night!" cried the young man, stepping back a pace.

K. said in a markedly quiet voice: "Let me tell you that I am the Land Surveyor whom the Count is expecting. My assistants are coming on to-morrow in a carriage with the apparatus. I did not want to miss the chance of a walk through the snow, but unfortunately lost my way several times and so arrived very late."

The young man said: "I'll ring up and enquire." There was a telephone in this village inn. The young man reported that he had found K. sleeping on a bag of straw. He had roused the man, questioned him, and duly warned him off the Count's territory. K. claimed to be a Land Surveyor engaged by the Count. He begged to enquire if a Land Surveyor were really expected.

Then there was silence while enquiries were made up there. The reply seemed brief enough, for the young man hung up the receiver immediately, crying angrily: "Just what I said! Not a trace of a Land Surveyor. A common, lying tramp, and probably worse."

But the telephone rang again, and with a special insistence. The young man listened to a fairly long statement, and then said in a low voice: "A mistake, is it? I'm sorry to hear that. The head of the department himself said so? Very queer. How am I to explain it all to the Land Surveyor?" So the Castle had recognised him as the Land Surveyor.

K. slept deeply until morning. After breakfast, he prepared to go out immediately into the village. As he was going out he noticed a dark portrait on the wall. "Who is that?" asked K., "the Count?" "No," said the landlord, "the Castellan." "A handsome castellan, indeed," said K., "a pity that he has such an ill-bred son." The landlord whispered in his ear: "Schwarzer exaggerated yesterday, his father is only an under-castellan, and one of the lowest, too."

Now he could see the Castle above him, clearly defined in the glittering air, its outline made still more definite by the moulding of snow covering it in a thin layer. On the whole this distant prospect of the Castle satisfied K.'s expectations. It was a rambling pile consisting of innumerable small buildings closely packed together.

But on approaching it he was disappointed in the Castle; it was after all only a wretched-looking town, a huddle of village houses, whose sole merit, if any, lay in being built of stone, but the plaster had long since flaked off and the stone seemed to be crumbling away.

Again K. came to a stop, as if in standing still he had more power of judgment. But he was disturbed. The children were just coming out with their teacher. Being the stranger, K. made the first advance and said, "Good morning, sir." "You are looking at the Castle?" he asked more gently than K. had expected, but with an inflection that denoted disapproval of K.'s occupation. "Strangers never do," said the teacher.

K. was disconcerted, irritated by the conversation. He felt irresistibly drawn to seek out new acquaintances, but each new acquaintance only seemed to increase his weariness. So he resumed his walk, but the way proved long. For the street did not lead up to the Castle hill, it only made towards it and then, as if deliberately, turned aside.

He made a snowball and threw it at a window. The door opened immediately, and there appeared an old peasant. K. stumbled over a washing-tub, a woman's hand steadied him. "I am the Count's Land Surveyor," said K., trying to justify himself. "Oh, it's the Land Surveyor," said a woman's voice.

From a large opening, a pale snowy light came in, apparently from the courtyard, and gave a gleam as of silk to the dress of a woman who was almost reclining in a high arm-chair. She was suckling an infant at her breast. She made a beautiful, sad, fixed picture. "Who are you?" asked K., and she replied: "A girl from the Castle."

K. was soon out in the street. Snow was again falling, yet the sky seemed a little brighter. Just then in the hut on his left hand a tiny window was opened, and a man asked, not unamiably, but still as if he were anxious to have no complications: "Are you waiting for somebody?" "For a sledge, to pick me up," said K. "If you like, I'll take you in my sledge." "Please do," said K. delighted. "Where do you want to be taken?" "To the Castle," returned K. quickly. "I won't take you there," said the man without hesitation. "Oh, well, take me to the inn," said K.

When by a turn in the road K. recognised that they were near the inn, he was greatly surprised to see that darkness had already set in. At the top of the little flight of steps leading into the house stood the landlord, holding up a lighted lantern. Not until he was level with the landlord did he notice two men, one on either side of the doorway. It was the men he had already met, who were called Arthur and Jeremiah. They now saluted him. "Who are you?" he asked. "Your assistants," they answered. "It's your assistants," corroborated the landlord.

"I am the Land Surveyor," said K., "and to-day I begin my work." He then gave his assistants some instructions. He said: "I'll call you both Arthur. If I tell Arthur to go anywhere you must both go, if I give Arthur something to do you must both do it." He then told them to get a sleigh for taking him to the Castle and have it ready outside at six o'clock. They said it could not be done, no stranger can get into the Castle without a permit.

K. said he would telephone himself. The receiver gave out a buzz of a kind that K. had never before heard on a telephone. It was like the hum of countless children's voices—blended by sheer impossibility into one high but resonant sound. A conversation ensued with an official named Oswald. K. announced himself as the Land Surveyor's assistant. The official asked: "What Land Surveyor? What assistant?" The answer came: "Oh, yes. That everlasting Land Surveyor. Quite so."

After K. had finished telephoning, a young man pushed his way through the peasants. He was clothed nearly all in white, not in silk, of course; he was in winter clothes like all the others, but the material he was wearing had the softness and dignity of silk. His face was clear and frank, his eyes larger than ordinary. His smile was unusually joyous. "Who are you?" asked K. "My name is Barnabas," said he, "I am a messenger." He handed K. a letter.

The letter's contents were as follows: "My dear Sir, As you know, you have been engaged for the Count's service. Your immediate superior is the Superintendent of the village, who will give you all particulars about your work and the terms of your employment, and to whom you are responsible. I myself, however, will try not to lose sight of you. Barnabas, the bearer of this letter, will report himself to you from time to time to learn your wishes and communicate them to me. You will find me always ready to oblige you, in so far as that is possible. I desire my workers to be contented." The signature was illegible, but stamped beside it was "Chief of Department X."

K. considered that only as a worker in the village, removed as far as possible from the sphere of the Castle, could he hope to achieve anything in the Castle itself. He would become a village worker with a distinctive but merely apparent connection with the Castle.

K. went back to the inn and told Barnabas: "Convey to the Chief my thanks for his recognition and for his great kindness. I shall follow his instructions faithfully." He asked Barnabas to take him to the Castle. Barnabas led him through the night to his own home, not to the Castle. K. was bitterly disappointed. He had been bewitched by Barnabas' close-fitting, silken-gleaming jacket, which, now that it was unbuttoned, displayed a coarse dirty grey shirt patched all over, and beneath that the huge muscular chest of a labourer.

K. went with Barnabas's sister Olga to another inn, called the Herrenhof, reserved exclusively for gentlemen from the Castle. In the bar, a young girl called Frieda drew the beer. An unobtrusive little girl with fair hair, sad eyes and hollow cheeks, but with a striking look of conscious superiority. As soon as her eye met K.'s it seemed to him that her look decided something concerning himself.

Frieda pointed to a peephole where K. could see Herr Klamm. At a desk in the middle of the room in a comfortable arm-chair sat Herr Klamm, his face brilliantly lit up by an incandescent lamp. A middle-sized, plump and ponderous man. "Do you know Herr Klamm well?" K. asked Frieda. "Oh, yes," she said, "very well. I am his mistress." "Klamm's mistress," said K. She nodded.

K. said to Frieda: "You ought to leave Klamm and become my sweetheart." She asked when she could talk to him. He asked if he could spend the night there. She told him to go out first with Olga, so that she could clear out all the others, and then he could come back. K. went out with Olga, then returned. When the landlord came in looking for K., Frieda hid him under the counter.

Frieda turned out the electric light and was under the counter beside K. "My darling! My darling!" she whispered. They embraced each other, her little body burned in K.'s hands, and they rolled a little way, landing with a thud on Klamm's door. From Klamm's room a deep, authoritative impersonal voice called for Frieda. "I'm with the Land Surveyor!" she cried.

K. stayed in bed the whole day through, and the whole night. When he got up at last on the following morning, it was the fourth day since his arrival in the village. K. said to Frieda: "What I would like best would be for Frieda and myself to get married, the sooner the better." The landlady of the Bridge Inn came to them and said: "There's only the question now of what guarantees you are to give Frieda."

K. said: "Before I'm married there's something I must do. I must have a talk with Klamm." "That's impossible," said Frieda. "Why is it impossible?" said K. The landlady explained: "Klamm doesn't talk even to people from the village, never yet has he spoken a word himself to anyone in the village."

The landlady continued: "You've snatched Frieda from the happiest state she had ever known. Frieda has given up all she had for the pleasure of sitting on your knee." K. said: "I shall now go and discuss this and other business with the Superintendent."

K. went to see the Superintendent. The Superintendent, a kindly, stout, clean-shaven man, was laid up with gout. K. read out Klamm's letter. The Superintendent said: "You've been taken on as Land Surveyor, as you say, but, unfortunately, we have no need of a Land Surveyor. There wouldn't be the least use for one here. The frontiers of our little state are marked out and all officially recorded. So what should we do with a Land Surveyor?"

The Superintendent told a long, complicated story about how the summons had come about through an error in the bureaucratic system, involving many departments and officials over many years. "A Control Official discovered meanwhile that a query had gone out from Department A to the Town Council many years before regarding a Land Surveyor, without having received a reply up till then. A new enquiry was sent to me, and now the whole business was really cleared up."

K. asked: "But now I'm here, and you know it. Are there no other spheres of activity in which I might be employed?" The Superintendent replied: "The only thing I will not agree to is that you should be taken on as Land Surveyor. Klamm's letter is valuable and worthy of respect, but it is in no sense an official letter, but only a private letter. It means nothing more than that Klamm intends to take a personal interest in you if you should be taken into the state service."

K. said: "I don't want any act of favour from the Castle, but my rights." He then took his leave. Before the inn the landlord was waiting for him, saying his wife was very bothered and unhappy on his account. K. went to see her. She lay in bed and showed him a photograph of Klamm. She told him the story of her connection with Klamm years before. She had been Klamm's mistress. Klamm had sent for her three times, but never a fourth time. She had then married the landlord and taken over the inn.

K. then went to the Herrenhof again. He learned that Klamm was still there, his sledge waiting in the yard. K. went into the courtyard. He saw the sledge. He opened the door and slipped inside. The warmth within the sledge was extraordinary. He pulled out a flask of brandy from the side-pocket and took a sip. Then everything became bright, the electric lights blazed. A gentleman came slowly out of the house. It was not Klamm, but a young man named Erlanger.

Erlanger said: "This is unheard of." K. was led into the taproom where Momus, Klamm's village secretary, was sitting with papers. Momus asked K. to submit to an examination. K. refused. The landlady urged him to comply, saying this protocol was his only connection with Klamm. K. still refused and left.

On the way back to the school, Barnabas met him with another letter from Klamm. The letter read: "The surveying work which you have carried out thus far has been appreciated by me. The work of the assistants too deserves praise. Do not slacken in your efforts! Carry your work on to a fortunate conclusion. For the rest be easy in your mind; the question of salary will presently be decided. I shall not forget you."

K. realized there had been a misunderstanding. He had done no surveying at all. He gave Barnabas a message for Klamm: "The Land Surveyor begs the Director to grant him a personal interview; he accepts in advance any conditions which may be attached to the permission to do this."

K. returned to the school, where Frieda was waiting. They had a difficult night, with the assistants causing trouble. The next morning the teacher and the lady teacher arrived with the children. There was conflict over the condition of the schoolroom. The teacher gave K. notice, but K. refused to accept it, saying only the Superintendent could dismiss him.

Later, a boy named Hans Brunswick came to see K. He was the son of the woman K. had seen at Lasemann's, who came from the Castle. They arranged that Hans would take K. to see his mother in two days' time, when his father would be away. Frieda became jealous and suspicious of K.'s interest in the family.

Frieda confronted K. with the landlady's accusations: that he only wanted her because she had been Klamm's mistress, and that he was using her as a means to reach Klamm. K. defended himself, but Frieda was deeply troubled. She confessed that the assistants had been pursuing her and that she was drawn to them.

K. went to Barnabas's house to look for him. Olga told him the long story of their family's disgrace. Three years ago, at a Fire Brigade celebration, an official named Sortini had seen Amalia and sent her an insulting letter demanding she come to him. Amalia tore up the letter and threw it in the messenger's face. Since then, the whole family had been ostracized by the village and ignored by the Castle.

Olga explained how their father had tried in vain to obtain forgiveness, wearing himself out with endless petitions until he became an invalid. Their mother also fell into illness and weakness. Olga herself had gone to the Herrenhof and associated with the servants to try to find the messenger and make amends, sacrificing her own reputation. Barnabas had become a messenger in hopes of gaining favour for the family.

K. listened to the whole story. He felt a bond with this family in their struggle against the inscrutable powers of the Castle. He promised to help them if he could. Then an assistant came looking for him at Frieda's command. K. avoided him and went out through the garden.

In the street, K. met Jeremiah, who told him that Frieda had left him and was now with Jeremiah. They had returned to the Herrenhof, where Frieda was back in the taproom. Jeremiah said that Arthur had gone to the Castle to complain about K.'s treatment of them. K. was stunned but tried to remain calm.

Barnabas arrived with news. He had not been able to give K.'s message to Klamm, but he had met Erlanger, one of Klamm's chief secretaries, who was going to the Herrenhof and wanted to see K. immediately in room number 15. K. set off at once for the Herrenhof, with Jeremiah following.

At the Herrenhof, a group of men were waiting outside for Erlanger. They complained about having to stand in the snow. Finally Momus appeared and called for Gerstacker and K. to be admitted. In the passage, K. saw Frieda. They spoke. Frieda accused him of going to the Barnabas girls. K. defended himself, saying he went only for Barnabas's messages.

Frieda said there would be no marriage. She was with Jeremiah now. K. argued that Jeremiah was only using her, that his feelings were cold and calculating. Frieda seemed to waver, but then Jeremiah appeared, shivering and ill, and Frieda went to him. She led him away, closing the door on K.

K. was left alone in the passage, facing another defeat in his struggle. The novel breaks off at this point, though Kafka's friend Max Brod adds a note that Kafka told him the ending: K. was to continue striving until he died of exhaustion, and at his death, word was to come from the Castle that though he had no legal claim to live in the village, yet, taking certain auxiliary circumstances into account, he was permitted to live and work there.
